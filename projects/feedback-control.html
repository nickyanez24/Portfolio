CONTROL SYSTEMS

Aircraft Feedback Control

“Skyhawk Stabilizer” feedback controller for pitch recovery and altitude regulation in X-Plane.

Overview

This project was completed for Feedback Control Systems with one other student. The goal was to design and implement a feedback controller that could recover a Cessna-style aircraft from a pitch disturbance in X-Plane while reducing long-term altitude drift.

The aircraft did not naturally return to steady flight after a pitch impulse. Without control, the pitch response deviated significantly and the altitude continued to drift. We tested multiple control strategies before selecting a final architecture that combined pitch PD control with vertical velocity feedback.

Engineering Goal

Design a controller that uses elevator input to recover from a pitch impulse, regulate vertical motion, and bring the aircraft back toward stable flight.

Control Problem

A pitch disturbance affects more than pitch angle. It also changes vertical velocity, which then changes altitude over time. A controller that only corrects pitch can make the attitude response look better while still allowing the aircraft to drift vertically.

That made the project a real feedback design problem. The controller needed to reject the disturbance quickly, but not so aggressively that it caused oscillation, overcorrection, or unrealistic elevator commands.

Baseline Flight Behavior

The baseline case showed why a controller was needed. After the pitch impulse, the aircraft response was not naturally stable. The pitch reached a much larger peak response, and the altitude continued drifting instead of returning cleanly toward steady flight.

Design Process

We did not jump straight to the final controller. We tested several strategies and used the failures to guide the final design. Direct altitude control was slow and difficult to tune. Pitch-only PD control improved pitch recovery, but altitude still drifted. Integral control was added to reduce steady-state error, but it caused instability and overcorrection.

The final design used pitch PD control plus vertical velocity feedback. That gave the controller a way to respond to altitude trends earlier, instead of only reacting after the aircraft had already drifted away.

Final Design Feature

Final Control Architecture

The final controller used pitch feedback, pitch-rate feedback, and vertical velocity feedback. The controller was implemented in Python to interface with X-Plane, while MATLAB was used to process flight data and generate the pitch and altitude response plots.

The final gains were Kp = 0.35, Kd = 0.18, and Kv = 0.0018. The design also used filtered feedback signals and elevator command saturation so the controller would behave more realistically in simulation.

Controller Term Gain Purpose
Pitch proportional feedback Kp = 0.35 Corrects pitch error after the disturbance.
Pitch-rate derivative feedback Kd = 0.18 Reduces aggressive motion and improves damping.
Vertical velocity feedback Kv = 0.0018 Reduces long-term altitude drift by responding to vertical motion.

Results

The final controller improved the aircraft response compared to the baseline. The controlled pitch response recovered more cleanly after the impulse, and the altitude response showed reduced long-term drift compared to the uncontrolled case.

The final design was not chosen because it worked in one isolated test. It came from comparing multiple controller architectures and selecting the one that best balanced pitch recovery, altitude regulation, smoothness, and stability.

Engineering Takeaways

This project helped me understand why controller architecture matters just as much as gain tuning. Directly controlling altitude seemed intuitive, but it did not produce a clean response. Pitch-only control improved attitude behavior, but it did not fully solve altitude drift. Adding integral control made the response worse by overcorrecting.

The biggest takeaway was learning how pitch, vertical velocity, and altitude interact dynamically. The final controller worked better because it responded to vertical motion directly while still stabilizing pitch, which made the aircraft response smoother and more stable in simulation.
