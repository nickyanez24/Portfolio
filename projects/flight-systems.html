ROCKET HARDWARE

Flight Systems Hardware

High-powered rocket manufacturing, recovery integration, OpenRocket stability analysis, and launch operations with Longhorn Rocketry.

Overview

As a member of Longhorn Rocketry's Flight Systems Hardware team, I supported the manufacturing, assembly, integration, and launch preparation of high-powered rockets. This role gave me hands-on experience with the practical side of aerospace hardware: building components, checking fit, integrating subsystems, preparing recovery hardware, and supporting rockets through test and competition operations.

This was one of my first real introductions to flight hardware outside of class. I learned how much work happens between a design being “done” and a rocket being ready to launch. Manufacturing, tolerances, recovery checks, stability analysis, and pad operations all had to come together before the vehicle could fly.

Role Context

I worked on the hardware side of Longhorn Rocketry, supporting rocket manufacturing and integration while also using OpenRocket to evaluate vehicle stability, center of pressure behavior, and expected flight performance.

Hands-On Rocket Manufacturing

A lot of the work started with learning how to build and prepare rocket hardware at the subscale level. That included sanding, cutting, drilling, fitting, bonding, fin attachment, and airframe preparation. These tasks were not just practice work. They built the manufacturing skills needed to understand how high-powered rocket components actually come together.

Working on subscale rockets helped me understand why tolerances, surface preparation, alignment, and bonding quality matter. Even simple hardware tasks can affect whether a rocket assembles cleanly, flies straight, and survives launch and recovery.

Subscale rocket manufacturing work used to build hands-on experience with sanding, bonding, fin attachment, and airframe preparation.

I also supported recovery system preparation and verification. Recovery hardware is one of the most important parts of a high-powered rocket because a successful launch still depends on safe deployment and recovery after apogee.

Recovery System Ejection Testing

Recovery testing was one of the most important parts of the hardware process. The parachute system had to deploy reliably, which meant testing the ejection behavior before flight. This kind of test helped verify that the recovery system could separate and deploy as intended instead of only assuming it would work during launch.

This was a good example of how rocket hardware needs physical validation. A recovery design can look correct on paper, but the actual system still needs to be tested with the hardware, packing method, and deployment setup that will be used for flight.

Ejection test used to verify parachute deployment behavior and recovery system readiness before flight operations.

OpenRocket Stability Analysis

In addition to shop and integration work, I used OpenRocket to evaluate vehicle stability and expected flight behavior. This included checking the vehicle configuration, center of gravity, center of pressure, stability margin, and projected performance.

This analysis helped connect the physical rocket hardware to the flight behavior we cared about. The rocket could not just be assembled correctly. It also needed enough stability margin and a reasonable performance prediction before launch.

OpenRocket model used to evaluate the competition vehicle configuration, CG/CP locations, stability margin, predicted apogee, and max flight conditions.

Test Launch Preparation

I also supported launch preparation for larger rocket systems. Seeing the competition rocket staged on the rail made the connection between shop work and flight operations much more real. Every integration decision, recovery check, and stability assumption eventually has to survive the launch environment.

Test launch campaigns were useful because they exposed the team to pad procedures, launch readiness, and the discipline needed before a rocket can be safely flown.

Competition rocket staged on the launch rail during a test launch campaign.

Spaceport America Cup Operations

I was selected as one of 17 members to attend Spaceport America Cup with Longhorn Rocketry. Being part of that environment helped me understand how much preparation goes into a competition rocket beyond the design work itself.

At competition, the work becomes about readiness: hardware checks, pad operations, documentation, launch procedures, recovery planning, and making sure every subsystem is prepared before flight. That experience gave me a better appreciation for how much integration discipline matters in aerospace projects.

Engineering Takeaways

This experience helped me understand how much engineering happens between a design and a launch-ready rocket. Even simple manufacturing and integration steps require attention to tolerances, alignment, hardware interfaces, and assembly order.

The biggest takeaway was learning that flight hardware is unforgiving. A rocket can look good in CAD or analysis, but the real system still has to be built, checked, integrated, tested, and prepared carefully before it can fly.
